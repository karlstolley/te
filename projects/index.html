Project One: Basic Marking and Copyediting Due Wednesday, February 15, 11:59pm CT

Project Description

For your first project, you will choose a Wikipedia article identified during November 2016 or December 2016 as needing copyediting. You may choose any article to copyedit, provided that it:

  • provides coverage of a non-technical topic. For example, the entry for Tavis Smiley would be a non-technical example, versus the entry for CoffeeScript.
  • is at least 1500 words, not including references. Copy and paste the main text into a word processor to get a rough word count.
  • is primarily made up of paragraph-like text, meaning that the article is not a list, timeline, or other short-form text.

You will create a PDF of the copy, and mark it using the basic editorial symbols covered in Chapter 2 of The Copyeditor’s Handbook (33-35).

Project Goals

  • Learn to create markable copy from web pages.
  • Learn to edit non-technical copy with a general audience in mind.
  • Mark copy using traditional editorial symbols outlined in The Copyeditor’s Handbook.
  • Follow the guidance of an in-house style manual. In this case, Wikipedia's Manual of Style.
  • Create a style sheet that governs internal consistency within the article you are marking.

Deliverables & Milestones

  1. An email to the instructor containing the initial deliverables (by Friday, January 20). No two students may mark the same article, so do this as soon as possible:
    • The URL to your chosen Wikipedia article marked as needing copyedits as of November 2016 or December 2016
    • The Dropbox share link link to your copyediting-friendly PDF
  2. An email to the instructor containing the project’s final deliverables (by Wednesday, February 15, 11:59pm CT):
    • A Dropbox share link pointing to the PDF of your scanned, marked copy
    • A Dropbox or Google Docs link to your style sheet derived from problems and inconsistencies in the article
    • A 4-5 sentence self-critique memo of your project and your progress in class to this point.

Requirements

  • Your chosen article must:
    • cover a non-technical topic
    • have running copy of approximately 1500-2000 words
    • be primarily paragraph-length content.
  • Your marked article copy must be:
    • Prepared in PDF format. One way to do this is to intall a readability or print-friendly addon/extension for your web browser. Create the PDF based on the output from your readability add-on of choice. Although Wikipedia provides the ability to download PDFs of their articles, you should learn to create copyediting-friendly PDFs from any website, using your own tools. We will have a Basecamp thread to discuss possible browsers and add-ons.
    • Printed and marked with a pen or pencil and scanned, or marked digitally with drawing tools that allow you to create the marks described in The Copyeditor’s Handbook
    • Marked according to the Wikipedia Manual of Style
    • Submitted to the instructor in final, marked form as a Dropbox link to your PDF.

Project Two: Copyediting Technical Material Due Wednesday, March 22, 11:59pm CT

Project Description

For this project, the instructor will assign you an open-source software project’s web-based technical manual and project website, both of which you will edit for content and basic formatting. You will also create a short in-house style guide for the software project’s manual and promotional website.

Project Three: Editing for Academic Publication Due Wednesday, May 3, 11:59pm CT

Project Description

You will edit a pre-publication academic manuscript for Project Three, including formatting its citations and reference list according to the Chicago Manual of Style. While you will of course be working on sentence-level edits, keep in mind also issues of style, clarity, and the consistency and overall organization of the manuscript. Query the author frequently; academics are a special breed when it comes to feeling protective of their writing.
